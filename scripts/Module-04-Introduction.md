# Module 4: Exterior Finishes & Building Envelope
## Introduction Script — Narrator: J.P.

---

[look directly at camera, composed]

Welcome back. I'm J.P., and this is Module Four — Exterior Finishes and the Building Envelope.

[pause]

We've prepped the site, poured the foundation, and framed the structure. You can stand inside and see the bones. But right now, the house is completely exposed — rain, wind, humidity, temperature swings. Until we seal this building up, we can't start interior trades. That's what this module is about.

[deliberate tone]

The building envelope separates interior conditioned space from the exterior environment. Roofing, siding, windows, doors, weather-resistant barriers, flashing — all working as an integrated system. Not individual products. A system.

[lean in]

And here's the key — water is the number one enemy of a house. Not wind, not temperature. Bulk water intrusion, vapor diffusion, condensation — these cause rot, mold, structural damage. The building envelope is your defense, and it has to be installed correctly, in the right sequence, with the right materials.

[gesture outward]

First — roofing. Your primary defense against bulk water from above. Underlayment systems, shingle installation, flashing at valleys and penetrations, drip edge, ventilation requirements. There's a layered system underneath that has to function correctly, and sequencing matters.

Second — siding and exterior cladding. Vinyl, fiber cement, engineered wood, stone veneer — the principles are the same. Behind the cladding, you need a properly installed weather-resistant barrier and drainage plane that lets water escape. Improper installation traps moisture inside the wall cavity — exactly where you don't want it.

[look at camera]

Third — windows and doors. The most vulnerable points in the entire envelope. Every opening is a potential leak path. Head flashing, sill pan flashing, jamb sealing, integration with house wrap — these details are non-negotiable.

Fourth — moisture management at the foundation level. How the above-grade envelope ties into below-grade waterproofing, how grade transitions are sealed. These transitions are where failures happen.

[conversational]

The inspector covers everything from roof nailing patterns to kickout flashing. We'll walk through the criteria so you know what to verify.

[gesture to self]

The CM's role is coordination-heavy — roofers, siding crews, window installers, all overlapping. Sequencing so trades aren't stepping on each other, materials staged, weather delays accounted for. You can't install siding in a downpour or leave a partially roofed house open overnight.

[slight nod]

The building envelope makes a house a shelter. Let's button it up.
