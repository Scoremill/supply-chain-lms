# Module 7: Interior Finishes
## Introduction Script — Narrator: J.P.

---

[look directly at camera, warm but purposeful]

Welcome back. I'm J.P., and this is Module Seven — Interior Finishes.

[slight smile]

This is where the house starts to feel like a home. Up to now, we've been building structure, installing systems, closing up walls. Important work, but invisible to the homeowner. Interior finishes are what people see, touch, and judge the entire build by. The standard for quality just went up significantly.

[more deliberate]

I've seen it happen — a builder does excellent structural work, nails the rough-in, gets clean inspections — then the finishes are sloppy. Paint runs. Trim gaps. Cabinets not level. And suddenly the homeowner questions the whole house. Fair or not, finish work is your calling card.

[gesture outward]

Four major trades transform bare drywall into a finished living space.

First — painting. Professional painting involves surface prep, priming, caulking, multiple coats, and standards that vary by surface. Brush, roll, and spray application — each has its place. Sheen levels matter — flat on a bathroom wall is a maintenance nightmare, high-gloss on a ceiling shows every flaw.

Second — trim carpentry. Base molding, crown, casing, built-ins, stair components. Material selection, coping versus mitering, and quality standards for fit and finish. Trim work is unforgiving — gaps show, misaligned miters catch the eye, paint won't hide a bad joint.

[look at camera]

Third — cabinet installation. One of the highest-cost finish items. Level, plumb, properly secured, consistent reveals, aligned doors. No wall is perfectly straight and no floor perfectly level, so the installer shims and fine-tunes every cabinet. Get cabinets wrong and the countertop won't fit.

Fourth — flooring. Hardwood, luxury vinyl, tile, or carpet — each has its own installation requirements, acclimation needs, and subfloor prep standards. Flatness tolerances, moisture testing, transition details, undercut casings — skip these steps and failures follow.

[conversational]

Here's what surprises people — sequencing matters as much as quality. Paint before or after trim? Cabinets before or after flooring? The CM makes that call. Wrong sequence means damage to finished surfaces and rework.

[gesture to self]

The CM's role shifts to quality management and schedule coordination — multiple trades in close proximity, protecting completed work, and conducting walkthroughs to catch defects before the homeowner sees them.

[slight nod]

Let's make it look right.
