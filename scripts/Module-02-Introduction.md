# Module 2: Foundation
## Introduction Script — Narrator: J.P.

---

[look directly at camera, steady and grounded]

Welcome back. I'm J.P., and this is Module Two of "How to Build a Home" — Foundation.

[pause, let it land]

If Module One was about preparing the stage, this one is about building the base everything sits on. Your foundation isn't just concrete in the ground — it's the structural backbone of the home. Get it right, and the build has a fighting chance. Get it wrong, and you'll chase problems for the life of the structure.

[more conversational]

I've seen cracked walls, doors that won't close, sloping floors — and nine times out of ten, it traces back to foundation. A footing that wasn't sized right. Waterproofing skipped. Drainage as an afterthought. These are structural failures that start right here.

[gesture outward]

We're breaking down the key trades involved in building a proper foundation — more moving parts than most people expect.

[counting off]

First — the excavator. Not as simple as firing up a backhoe. We'll cover hole excavation, what soil conditions dictate, and utility trenching — water, sewer, gas, and electrical routed before anything gets poured. Margin for error is slim.

Next — the concrete contractor. Footings, foundation walls, waterproofing, drainage tile, slab pours. Each has its own code requirements and quality standards. We'll cover what makes a good footing, wall construction — poured or block — and why waterproofing and drainage are code-required.

[lean in]

Then the plumber. Under-slab rough-in happens before that floor gets poured. Once concrete goes down, those pipes are buried — no going back without a jackhammer. Pipe sizing, drain slope, fixture locations, pressure testing — all must be precise. One of the most critical inspections on the project.

[straighten up]

The city inspector plays a major role too. We'll walk through the footing inspection and the under-slab inspection. A failed inspection means a schedule delay — and delays during foundation ripple through every phase after.

[gesture to self]

Then there's the construction manager — the orchestra conductor. Excavator, concrete crew, plumber, inspector — all on overlapping timelines. The CM manages that schedule, coordinates handoffs, verifies quality, and calls when to backfill.

[more serious]

Backfilling authorization is a big one. You don't backfill until conditions are right. We'll cover why jumping the gun causes catastrophic damage.

[look at camera]

This module is dense, but essential. The foundation is where you earn credibility as a builder. Let's dig in.

[hold gaze for two seconds, then fade]
