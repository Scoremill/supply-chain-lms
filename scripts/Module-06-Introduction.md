# Module 6: Insulation and Drywall
## Introduction Script — Narrator: J.P.

---

[look directly at camera, calm and steady]

Welcome back. I'm J.P., and this is Module Six — Insulation and Drywall.

[pause]

I know what some of you might be thinking — how complicated can it be? Stuff the batts in and screw some sheets up, right?

[shake head]

Wrong. That thinking is exactly why so many homes underperform on energy efficiency and develop moisture problems the homeowner can't explain. This is where building science meets construction practice. If you don't understand the science, you'll make mistakes hidden behind walls for the life of the structure.

[more serious]

After MEPs rough-in, every cavity is wide open — framing, wiring, plumbing, ductwork, all visible. Once insulation and drywall go in, you're never seeing those cavities again unless something fails. This is your last chance to get the thermal and air barrier right. No do-overs.

[gesture outward]

Two major trades transform the house from open structure to sealed living space.

First — insulation. Way beyond filling cavities. We'll cover how heat moves through conduction, convection, and radiation. Different types — fiberglass batts, blown cellulose, spray foam, rigid board — and where each is appropriate.

[lean in]

What really matters is installation quality. An R-19 batt that's compressed or gapped might perform at R-11 or worse. Grade One installation means full contact with all six sides of the cavity — no gaps, no voids, no compression, proper vapor retarder. That's the standard the inspector verifies.

We'll also cover air sealing — arguably more important than insulation itself. R-40 walls mean nothing if air leaks through penetrations and unsealed rim joists. Air sealing and insulation work as a system.

[straighten up]

Second — drywall. Gypsum board gives the house interior surfaces, but it's also critical for fire-resistance and sound assemblies required by code. Board selection matters — moisture-resistant in wet areas, fire-rated on garage walls, standard everywhere else. Hanging techniques, fastener patterns, and the finishing process — taping, mudding, sanding, Levels One through Five.

[look at camera]

Texture and finish quality affect the final paint job. Defects show under paint, especially higher sheens. Get it right here to avoid rework.

[conversational]

The insulation inspection happens before drywall — R-values, vapor retarders, air sealing. Fail it, and your drywall schedule gets pushed.

The CM manages sequencing — inspection passed before drywall delivery, proper staging, timeline through hanging, finishing, drying. Mud needs cure time — rushing leads to cracking.

[slight nod]

Let's seal it up and finish it out.
